          Claim vs Opinion: A Statistical Analysis of User Engagement
                         on TikTok Video Content
================================================================================

1. INTRODUCTION AND PROBLEM STATEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Short-form video platforms such as TikTok have become major channels for
information dissemination, hosting both factual claims and personal opinions.
Understanding whether content type influences user engagement is critical for
platform governance and misinformation research. This study addresses the
following core question:

    "Does content type (claim vs. opinion) predict user engagement on TikTok?"

We employ two complementary statistical methods — probability distribution
fitting via Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) and hypothesis testing — to
systematically quantify the engagement difference between claim and opinion
videos. Additionally, Bayesian conditional probability analysis and logistic
regression are conducted as supplementary investigations.

The dataset used is the TikTok Video Dataset (sourced from Kaggle), containing
19,382 records with 12 variables. After removing 298 rows with missing values
in the numerical engagement fields, 19,084 valid observations remain, split
approximately equally between claim (9,608; 50.3%) and opinion (9,476; 49.7%)
videos.

3. METHOD 1: PROBABILITY DISTRIBUTION FITTING VIA MLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3.1 Distribution Selection

Given the non-negative, right-skewed nature of engagement count data, the
log-normal distribution is a natural candidate. If X follows a log-normal
distribution, i.e., X ~ LogNormal(μ, σ²), then Y = ln(X) follows a normal
distribution Y ~ N(μ, σ²).

The probability density function is:

    f(x; μ, σ) = 1/(x·σ·√(2π)) · exp{ -[ln(x) - μ]² / (2σ²) },  x > 0

3.2 MLE Derivation for the Log-Normal Distribution

Given a random sample x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ from LogNormal(μ, σ²), we define
yᵢ = ln(xᵢ), so that yᵢ ~ N(μ, σ²).

The log-likelihood function is:

    ℓ(μ, σ²) = -(n/2)·ln(2π) - (n/2)·ln(σ²) - (1/2σ²)·Σᵢ(yᵢ - μ)²

Taking the partial derivative with respect to μ and setting it to zero:

    ∂ℓ/∂μ = (1/σ²)·Σᵢ(yᵢ - μ) = 0

Solving yields the MLE for μ:

    μ̂ = (1/n)·Σᵢ ln(xᵢ)

This is simply the sample mean of the log-transformed data.

Taking the partial derivative with respect to σ² and setting it to zero:

    ∂ℓ/∂(σ²) = -n/(2σ²) + (1/2σ⁴)·Σᵢ(yᵢ - μ)² = 0

Solving yields the MLE for σ²:

    σ̂² = (1/n)·Σᵢ [ln(xᵢ) - μ̂]²

This is the sample variance of the log-transformed data (using denominator n
rather than n−1, consistent with the MLE framework).

3.3 Parameter Estimation Results

We apply the above MLE formulas to video_view_count for both groups, and
verify the analytical solutions numerically via the Nelder-Mead optimization
of the negative log-likelihood. The results agree to four decimal places.

3.4 Goodness-of-Fit Assessment

Q-Q plots (Figure 3) show that the log-transformed view counts approximate
a straight line against theoretical normal quantiles, supporting the
log-normal assumption. However, the Shapiro-Wilk test rejects normality
(W ≈ 0.83, p < 10⁻⁵⁸) for both groups. This is expected given the very
large sample sizes (n > 9,000), where even minor deviations from normality
become statistically significant. Practically, the log-normal model provides
a reasonable and interpretable approximation.


4. METHOD 2: HYPOTHESIS TESTING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.1 Test Design

We test whether the engagement distributions of claim and opinion videos
differ significantly. Two complementary tests are employed:

  (a) Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric): Tests whether the two groups
      have the same distribution, without assuming normality.

  (b) Two-sample t-test on log-transformed data: Tests whether the means
      of log(view_count) differ between groups, assuming approximate
      normality after transformation.

For both tests:
  H₀: The engagement distribution (or log-mean) is the same for claim
      and opinion videos.
  H₁: The distributions (or log-means) differ.
  Significance level: α = 0.05

4.2 Results for video_view_count

Table 5. Hypothesis Test Results for View Count
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Test                    Statistic          p-value        Conclusion
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mann-Whitney U          U = 90,639,870     p ≈ 0          Reject H₀
Two-sample t (log)      t = 322.02         p ≈ 0          Reject H₀
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Levene's test for equality of variances on the log-transformed data yields
W = 0.050, p = 0.822, supporting the assumption of equal variances. The
Student t-test (rather than Welch's) is therefore appropriate.

Both tests decisively reject the null hypothesis. The effect size,
measured by Cohen's d on the log scale, is d = 4.66 — an extremely large
effect by conventional standards (d > 0.8 is considered "large").

4.4 Interpretation

The hypothesis tests provide overwhelming evidence that claim videos attract
substantially higher user engagement than opinion videos across all measured
dimensions. The consistency of results across both parametric and
non-parametric tests strengthens the robustness of this conclusion.


5. SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS: BAYESIAN CONDITIONAL PROBABILITIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5.1 Motivation

The dataset includes author_ban_status (active / banned / under review),
which allows us to examine the relationship between content type and account
standing using Bayesian reasoning.

5.2 Conditional Probability Computation

From the data, we compute:

    P(claim | active)        = 0.4268    (n = 15,383)
    P(claim | banned)        = 0.8801    (n = 1,635)
    P(claim | under review)  = 0.7759    (n = 2,066)

Banned accounts are more than twice as likely to produce claim content
compared to active accounts. Using Bayes' theorem, we can reverse the
conditioning:

    P(active | claim)        = 0.6834
    P(banned | claim)        = 0.1498
    P(under review | claim)  = 0.1668

5.3 Chi-Square Test of Independence

A chi-square test on the claim_status × author_ban_status contingency table
yields χ² = 1902.59, df = 2, p ≈ 0. The strong association suggests that
claim content is disproportionately linked to accounts with adverse standing
(banned or under review).

5.4 Verified Status Analysis

Similarly, verification status shows a strong association:

    P(claim | not verified)  = 0.5256    (n = 17,884)
    P(claim | verified)      = 0.1742    (n = 1,200)

Verified users are far less likely to post claim-type content, consistent
with the interpretation that claims may carry higher misinformation risk.

7. CONCLUSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This study investigated whether content type (claim vs. opinion) predicts
user engagement on TikTok, using a dataset of 19,084 videos. The findings
are unambiguous:

(1) Distribution Fitting: Video engagement metrics follow a log-normal
    distribution. MLE estimation reveals that claim videos have log-means
    (μ̂) approximately 4.6 units higher than opinion videos for view count,
    corresponding to a roughly 100-fold difference in the original scale.

(2) Hypothesis Testing: Both the Mann-Whitney U test and the two-sample
    t-test (on log-transformed data) decisively reject the null hypothesis
    of equal engagement (p ≈ 0 for all five metrics). Effect sizes are
    extremely large (Cohen's d > 2.8 for all variables).

(3) Bayesian Analysis: Claim content is strongly associated with banned
    accounts (P(claim|banned) = 0.88 vs. P(claim|active) = 0.43) and
    unverified users (P(claim|not verified) = 0.53 vs. P(claim|verified)
    = 0.17).

(4) Predictive Modeling: A simple logistic regression using only engagement
    features achieves 98.3% accuracy in classifying content type.

In conclusion, claim videos attract dramatically higher user engagement than
opinion videos across all measured dimensions. This finding has implications
for platform recommendation algorithms and content moderation policies, as
the incentive structure may disproportionately reward claim-type content —
including potential misinformation — with greater visibility.
